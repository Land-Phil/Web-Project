Showcase

This project is based on an optimization case study involving the allocation of medical linear accelerators (Linacs) to cancer treatment facilities in Ontario, Canada over a five-year period.

Linacs are commonly used in radiation therapy either as a curative treatment, to alleviate symptoms, or extend/improve quality of life. By customizing high energy x-rays or electrons to conform to a tumor's shape, the goal is to destroy cancer cells while minimizing damage to the surrounding normal tissue. Approximately half of cancer patients require radiation therapy at least once during the course of their illness, many requiring repeat treatments.

To meet the high demand for radiation treatment in Ontario, Cancer Care Ontario (CCO) launched a strategy which outlined recommendations for investments in linacs. A single linac can, on average, treat 426 patients per year. Several problems are associated with insufficient capacity: some patients may receive no treatment or may not receive it in a timely manner, patients may be required to travel to distant cancer centers at high personal cost, and some patients may be required to substitute other treatments that are not as effective as radiation therapy in their condition. Radiation therapy is often cost-effective but requires high initial investment. The capital resources and implementation timelines for the construction of new facilities were not realistic to meet current or near future demand. As such, CCO decided to fill current facilities before investing in new ones.

The facilities have three types of rooms: “treatment rooms” which are currently occupied by a linac, “empty rooms” which have been allocated for future expansion but aren’t currently in use, and “swing rooms” which are empty rooms typically used for equipment replacement. Once a linac has been installed it cannot be moved (excepting two portable linacs which CCO owns). Ideally empty rooms should be filled before swing rooms to maximize use of swing rooms for equipment replacement. Demand forecasts for radiotherapy were calculated based on the projected growth in cancer incidence across the province. Based on current system capacity, Ontario has a total of 16 empty or swing rooms that can potentially be filled with linacs over the next 5 years. Based on forecasted demand, the recommended allocation was as follows: 4 linacs in 2013, 3 linacs in 2014, 5 linacs in 2015, 2 linacs in 2016, 2 linacs in 2017. By 2018 CCO will run out of capacity and will require additional capital investments to build a new treatment facility.

My goal was to devise a strategy to allocate linacs to available rooms/facilities in a way that ensured quality radiation treatment to all patients across the province. In other words, I wanted to create a quantitative plan to meet demand, given the available resources and constraints. I determined the best route would be to optimize the ratio of patients per linac across all facilities (using Excel and an add-in called Solver) in order to provide an unbiased and data driven plan.
